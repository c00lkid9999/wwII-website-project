WWII and the Holocaust

1931: Japan invades Manchuria

Japan’s invasion of Manchuria increased tensions before World War II, and eventually caused the attack on Pearl Harbor, which got the United States involved in the war. This ultimately led to the Allies winning the war. It also paved the way for Japanese involvement in the war.

January 30, 1933: Hitler appointed German leader

Germany was severely unstable, and Hitler earned public favor by promising stability for Germany. If not for Hitler’s rise to power there may not have been a Second World War to begin with, as it was under his rule that Germany invaded Poland.

September 1, 1939: Germany invades Poland

Germany invading Poland marked the beginning of World War II, as both Great Britain and France declared war on Nazi Germany in response. To justify the action, the SS colluded with the German military to stage a phony attack on a German radio station, and accused the Poles of this attack.

September 28, 1939: Warsaw is Captured

Even though Britain and France declared war on Germany, the Soviet Union invaded Poland from the east on September 17. Warsaw officially surrendered after heavy shelling and bombing, the government having fled the same day that the Soviets entered the war.

December 13, 1939: UK wins battle of River Plate

The battle of River Plate was the first major naval battle of the Second World War, so the victory resulted in a major boost of morale for the UK. This came at great cost though as 72 allied soldiers and 36 German soldiers were lost.

May 10, 1940: Churchill becomes prime minister

Churchill had acquired a reputation for military leadership ability which is why he was chosen to replace Chamberlain as prime minister. Churchill was vital to the war because he succeeded in rallying the British people and led the UK from near defeat all the way to victory.

May 26 to June 4, 1940: Miracle of Dunkirk

The miracle of Dunkirk was a critical turning point in World War II, and it was only possible because Hitler exercised restraint by halting his panzer tanks for a couple of days shortly before the evacuation began. Hitler was hoping that the British would more readily negotiate if he gave them clemency, but instead the evacuation inspired national pride and gave the British momentum.

June 14, 1940: Paris falls to Germany

The fall of Paris was a severe blow to the Allied powers, as it meant the loss of France and one of the main forces fighting against Germany was inevitable. The decision to surrender Paris was made because France believed that resistance was not worth the destruction of such a beautiful and historic city, as it was a major symbol of French pride.

July 10 to Oct 31, 1940: Battle of Britain

The Battle of Britain was a crucial victory for the Allies, as Britain was able to hold the war in Western Europe and prevent Germany from invading Britain on the land or sea. Hitler hoped to weaken Britain’s morale by devastating it with aerial bombing, but Britain’s radar relays and technologically superior aircrafts prevented the German planes from having the destructive effect desired.

September 7, 1940 to May 11, 1941: The Blitz

The Blitz was a devastating period of 57 straight nights during which German bombers raided the city of London almost every night and sometimes during the day. Though the Blitz severely damaged London and harmed many people, it served to strengthen the British resolve to fight to the end of the war.

December 9, 1940 to February 9, 1941: Operation Compass

Operation Compass was an offensive in North Africa in which the Allied Western Desert Force pushed the Italian forces out of Egypt and Eastern Libya. Operation Compass granted the Allies control of the Western Desert, and though the Axis powers fought back, it was a much needed victory for the Allies after a long period of defeats.

June 22, 1941: Hitler launches Operation Barbarossa

Operation Barbarossa, a German invasion of the USSR, was delayed by five weeks due to the German invasion of Yugoslavia and Greece. Nevertheless, Hitler and the heads of the OKH (the German Army High Command), believed that they would be able to defeat the Red Army before the end of October, not realizing the many hindrances that the USSR could put in place, or that there would be an early winter that year in which many German troops would freeze.

December 7, 1941: Attack on Pearl Harbor

The Attack on Pearl Harbor involved three waves of heavy bombing from Japanese planes, each wave targeting a different area of the naval base. Though Japan was hoping to weaken the US navy and negotiate a quick peace, the US quickly declared war on Japan and joined the Allies. While the US was already providing support via resources, it now sent troops to fight as well.

January 20, 1942: Wannsee Conference

The Wannsee Conference was a meeting of high-level German officials to discuss the implementation of the “Final Solution to the Jewish Question”. None of the people at the meeting objected to the “Final Solution” which involved the systematic and deliberate killing of European Jews.

June 4 to 7, 1942: Battle of Midway

The Battle of Midway, fought almost entirely with aircraft, resulted in the United States destroying Japan’s carrier strength as well as some of Japan’s best trained naval pilots. The Battle of Midway ended the potential threat of further Japanese expansion in the Pacific Ocean.

November 8, 1942: US lands in North Africa

After much debate as to how to enter the war, President Roosevelt decided that the best course of action was to land in North Africa. This marked the start of American involvement in the war, and allowed the Allies to seize the offensive after a long period of the Axis powers dictating the war.

November 1942: Battle of Stalingrad

The Battle of Stalingrad resulted in a huge defeat for the Germans, and is often referred to as a major turning point in the war, as it stopped the German advance into the Soviet Union. Stalingrad would have been a huge prize for the German army, since it was a large industrial city producing armaments and tractors, but the Russian winter weakened and starved the German soldiers, and eventually the Germans at Stalingrad surrendered.

May 12, 1943: Axis surrenders in North Africa

The Axis forces in North Africa surrendered after being squeezed between the British and American forces, yielding 275,000 prisoners of war. This was a major loss of experienced troops for the Axis powers, and greatly reduced their military capacity.

August 1943: Allies take Sicily - September 3, 1943: Italy surrenders

The invasion of Sicily lasted for a mere 39 days, but it granted the Allies freedom to move around in the Mediterranean, as well as easy access to the Italian Peninsula itself. After the losses in North Africa and Sicily, the Fascist Grand Council voted for a resolution against Mussolini and he resigned his powers. The new government quickly entered secret negotiations with the allies, but the German forces in Italy still resisted the Allied invasion of mainland Italy.

June 6, 1944: D-Day

On D-Day, the allied land, air, and sea forces came together in the largest amphibious invasion in military history. The invasion landed on the shores of Normandy, France, and resulted in a victory for the Allies. D-Day enabled the liberation of France, causing Germany to lose out on the materials and manpower of France.

August 25, 1944: Paris liberated

On August 19, before the Allies even entered Paris, many French civilians banded together to fight back against the German occupiers. On August 24, Allied forces began to arrive in Paris, and the German forces surrendered on August 25 despite repeated orders that Paris “must not fall into enemy’s hand except lying in complete debris”.

December 16, 1944: Battle of the Bulge begins

The Battle of the Bulge was a German offensive that was meant to counter the Allied advance towards Germany. At first the battle went well for the German army, but the battle soon swayed in favor of the Allies. The failure of the Battle of the Bulge to deliver a devastating impact on the Allies ultimately spelled doom for the Germans, as it forfeited the chance of maintaining a prolonged resistance to the Allied offensive, and undermined the German morale.

April 28, 1945: Mussolini captured and executed

Benito Mussolini went to Milan after being deposed, but due to the Allied forces breaking into northern Italy he fled toward the Swiss border. He and his mistress were captured a couple of days later and executed the following afternoon, two days before Hitler’s suicide.

April 30, 1945: Hitler commits suicide - May 7, 1945: Germany surrenders

With it becoming increasingly clear that Germany would lose the war, Hitler committed suicide via a gunshot to the temple. Germany surrendered a week later, signaling the end of the war in Europe, and confirming the Naazi defeat.

August 6 - August 9, 1945: Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombings - August 14, 1945: Japanese surrender

The bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki is one of the most controversial decisions of the US government to date, as it brought death to many innocent civilians, but also quickly ended the war, possibly preventing the death of many more people. Whether or not it was the correct decision to bomb Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Japan surrendered within a week of the bombings, and World War Two was officially ended.
