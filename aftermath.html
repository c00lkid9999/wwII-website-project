The Aftermath of War

Europe and Germany

After the war, Germany and Austria were divided into occupation zones controlled by the Western Allies and the Soviet Union. Though the Western Allies and Soviet Union maintained good relations at first, their paths soon diverged, and West Germany and East Germany were only officially reunited in 1949. Austria was less fortunate and remained split until 1955. A denazification program in Germany led to the removal of ex-Nazis from power and the prosecution of Nazi war criminals. This denazification program eventually moved toward amnesty and reintegration of ex-Nazis into German society. The rest of Europe was also divided into western and eastern spheres of influence, with many eastern and central European countries developing communist-led regimes and becoming Soviet satellite states. This division was formalized with the Warsaw Pact and NATO, and it led to a long period of political and military tension between the United States and the Soviet Union. The global economy suffered greatly from the war, and the United States emerged much richer than the other nations. This wealth disparity was ameliorated with the Marshall plan, which extended economic aid to Western European countries, helping them to rebuild their infrastructure. The United Nations was also created after the war in order to prevent another from occurring. The United Nations adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights as a standard for all members in 1948, and fixed some of the problems that caused the League of Nations to fail.

Tensions between Europe and US

After World War Two, Germany was subdivided into four sections, each controlled by one of the four main Allied powers: the United States, the United Kingdom, France, and the Soviet Union. These nations made up the Allied Control Council. France used its position to obstruct policies that it thought wouldn’t benefit France, and pushed against anything that would lead to a unified German government. Relations with the Soviet Union also deteriorated, and each power ended up running its own administration in their zones. The British and American zones eventually merged into the Bizone, which the Soviets claimed was a violation of the Potsdam Agreement, and walked out of the council, thus incapacitating it. Residents of the Eastern Bloc, controlled by the Soviet Union, fled to West Germany due to a dislike of Soviet control and fear of further restrictions. This prompted East Germany to restrict its borders, eventually resorting to building the Berlin Wall, which grew to symbolize the political divide between the United States and the Soviet Union. This led to the next major chapter in American and world history: the cold war. Tensions between all of the major world powers that were previously involved in WWII were at their highest, and with the new threat of nuclear weapons, many people feared that if war broke out, it could be a nuclear war that would destroy many countries. It took many years for WWII tensions to subside, and some are still present in society today.

Discovery of the Holocaust

At first, images taken at liberated concentration camps were not publicly distributed, because the images taken were alarming and allied leadership wanted to avoid causing anyone who still had relatives that were missing to be alarmed. This policy of nondisclosure changed soon after American forces found the Ohrdruf concentration camp, where there was still a blazing bonfire with bodies inside and prisoners dead with bullet wounds in their heads. Upon the discovery of the camp, allied leadership immediately decided that the censorship about the concentration camps had to be lifted so that the world could see evidence of the Nazi atrocities and understand the horrors committed by the German soldiers.

The Marshall Plan

After the war, many European countries were starving, and the Secretary of State George C. Marshal was getting concerned, so he put together a plan that would be funded by the United States to provide relief and rebuild infrastructure. The plan provided capital and materials to Europe, enabling Europeans to rebuild their economy, and creating reliable trading partners. In contrast, the Treaty of Versailles which ended World War One focused on punishing Germany rather than aiding Europe. The Treaty of Versailles is often blamed for worsening the economic hardships of the time and ultimately laying the groundwork for the rise of the Nazi Party.

Bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki

On August 6 and 9, 1945, in one of the most controversial US government decisions in history, the United States detonated two atomic bombs over the Japanese towns of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, killing between 150,000 and 246,000 people. This is the only use of nuclear weapons in an armed conflict in history. Japan surrendered six days after the bombing, after the Soviet Union’s declaration of war against Japan, and after the invasion of Japanese-controlled Manchuria. This event effectively ended WWII, as the Japanese were the only Axis power that remained in the war. However, it also led to many new tensions throughout the world, as the dropping of the atomic bombs caused many countries to realize the full extent of what they could do and caused them to fear that they could be bombed. This led to the Cold War between all of the nuclear powers in the world, such as the United States and the USSR.

Post-War Relationship of US and Japan

After the surrender of Japan, the US began to help bring Japan back into the international community by strengthening their military, political, and economic ties. Eventually, treaties were signed with Japan over time and Japan became an important military ally of the United States. In addition, while the United States occupied Japan, they helped to enact reforms that enabled Japan to recover from the war and become a completely different society: a constitutional monarchy with a parliament and a drastically reduced military. This led to the Japan that we know today, which is much different than the pre-WWII Japan featuring an authoritarian government focused around an emperor that entered WWII.

Analysis: Post-war successes and failures

In the European theater of war, the post war was filled with many successes and failures. The rebuilding of Europe’s economy was a stark contrast to the failed treaty of Versailles, as it allowed for all countries to work together in rebuilding their economy rather than laying all of the blame on a single country. However, the post-war divisions of Germany led to the removal of many of the alliances between the US and the USSR, leading eventually to the cold war. In addition, the atrocities at concentration camps were initially kept from the world rather than being exposed to civilians so that they could see the atrocities that had occurred. In the pacific theater of war, the United States handled the rebuilding of their relationship with Japan well, resulting in a successful rebuilding of the Japanese government, economy, and more. This resulted in the United States being in good standing with Japan even through today. In all, the European and Pacific theaters of war both were very important to WWII. In both of them, there were many successes and failures, some of the most notable being the failure of the division of Germany but the success of the Marshall Plan. All of these led to many things, some of which are still present today, including the cold war, the US and Japan relationship, and more.
