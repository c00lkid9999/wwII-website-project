The Rise of Hitler, Antisemitism, and Nazism in Germany

Hitler's Rise to Power

After WWI, the allied powers charged the Weimar Republic with reparations that it was unable to fulfill, so it began to print money, causing hyperinflation. The cost of bread rose from 3 Reichsmarks in 1922 to 80 billion Reichsmarks in 1923. There was a recessionist movement which radicalized sections of the labor force. The national right in Bavaria, as a breach of the Weimar constitution, declared a state of emergency. No action was taken against Bavaria, though its leader was preparing a military coup. The German Workers’ party became the National Socialist German Workers’ Party in 1920, and Hitler named himself chairman of the party in 1921. After attempting his own coup, Hitler was sentenced to five years in prison. He served less than eight months, during which he wrote Mein Kampf. After this there was a period of five years called the “golden twenties” from 1924-1929. It featured an unstable political system, a softening of class fronts, and the “Young Plan” a plan to pay 2 billion Reichsmarks per year over 59 years in order to pay off reparations. This era soon ended with the Great Depression in 1930, which shook the German economy. A new finance expert then halted the economy and lowered wages and benefits, losing favor from the president, who replaced him with Adolf Hitler. In the election of July 1932, the Nazi Party won 37.3% of the vote. Since there was still no majority, a new election was scheduled. Kurt von Schleicher, a behind the scenes army general, maneuvered Papen out of the chancellor’s office in favor of himself. Amid rising fears about Schleicher deposing Hindenburg, Papen convinced him to appoint Hitler as chancellor.

Anti-Semitism

Antisemitism is thought to have originated in Egypt and been spread by the Greeks. An ancient Jewish philosopher from Alexandria described an attack on Jews in Alexandria in 38 CE which may have been caused by the Jews being misportrayed as misanthropes. Other arguments about the origin of antisemitism include that the cause was their separateness in the Greek cities, or that early hatred toward Jews weren’t anti semitic because many Greeks showed animosity toward anyone they deemed to be barbarous. In either case, many examples of anti-jewish sentiments can be found throughout Greece and ancient Rome, though Roman-Jewish relations had a period of increased tolerance from about 160 CE to the time that Christianity became the state religion. The late 6th century CE involved the Catholic Visigothic kingdom in Hispania issuing anti-Jewish laws which forbade Jews from marrying Christians or practicing many of their religious beliefs, imposing punishments ranging between forced conversion, slavery, exile, and death. There was then a golden age from the 9th century to at least the 11th century during which medieval Islamic countries offered Jews legal protection, though several Muslim pogroms against Jews were instated afterward. There were several Jewish massacres during the Crusades, followed by expulsions. Many of the expelled Jews fled to Poland. Throughout Renaissance Europe, the Franciscans and Dominicans combed Europe and promoted antisemitism through their fiery appeals. The Black Death was also blamed on Jews, and, though Pope Clement VI tried to protect them, many Jewish communities were destroyed. Martin Luther once wrote “...we are at fault in not slaying them…” when referring to the Jews. Antisemitism was brought to the United States as early as the 17th century, and New Amsterdam even implemented plans to keep Jews out of the city. Even Voltaire harbored a hatred of Jews. Catholic royalist Louis de Bonald called for the reversal of Jewish emancipation in the wake of the French Revolution, declaring Jews to be a “state within a state,” and that they should be forced to wear a distinctive mark to identify and discriminate against them. Many historians claim the 19th century was when the position of Jews in Muslim countries worsened. In 1850 Richard Wagner, “the inventor of modern antisemitism”, accused Jews of being harmful to German culture, corrupting morals and controlling the money economy. Many scholars view Karl Marx’s essay “On The Jewish Question” as antisemitic, though the subject is up for debate. From 1900 and 1924, many Jews migrated to America to escape pogroms. Hitler then rose to power with a vision of a “racially pure” Germany. The Nazis tried to concentrate Jews in camps or deport them, though this proved hard. Soon, anti-Jewish regulations were issued, including forced labor, the wearing of white armbands, and the seizure of most Jewish properties. The first ghettos were established in 1939, and often factories were operated from the labor of the ghetto inhabitants. Soon after began a systematic shooting of Jews, where the victims would undress and by shot into previously dug pits. Many were not killed by the gunfire but buried alive instead. Many groups besides jews were targeted as well, such as Slavs, non-Europeans, people with disabilities, homosexual men, and Romani.

Kristallnacht and Lebensraum

Krisallnacht, or the Night of Broken Glass, was a pogrom against Jews carried out by Nazis and the Sturmabteilung and Schutzstaffel, along with participation from the Hitler Youth and civilians that took place throughout Germany on November 9-10 in 1938. The pretext was the assassination of the German diplomat Ernst vom Rath by a Polish Jew. Jewish homes, hospitals, and schools were ransacked when rioters demolished buildings with sledgehammers, destroying 267 synagogues. Over 7,000- businesses were damaged or destroyed and 30,000 Jewish men were incarcerated. Lebensraum, which means living space, is a German concept of expansionism which became a geopolitical goal of Germany in World War I. It was also the leading motivator for Nazi Germany to initiate World War II, as the Nazi party supported the ultimate goal to establish a Greater German Reich. The Nazi party aimed to conquer Central and Eastern Europe and replace most of the populations there with Germans in the name of Lebensraum.

Hitler's Invasion of Poland

In order for Hitler to realize his goals of Lebensraum, he decided to attempt to conquer Poland. Hitler signed a treaty with the Soviets, highlighting their shared goals to conquer Poland and expand their territory in a secret agreement allotting each of them half of Poland if it was conquered. Hitler named this conflict “Case White.” He said of the invasion, “expanding our living space in the east and making our food supply secure; to have sufficient food you must have sparsely settled areas. There is therefore no question of sparing Poland, and the decision is to attack Poland at the first opportunity. We cannot expect a repetition of Czechoslovakia. There will be fighting.” Hitler planned to decimate the Polish population, especially the Jews, during his invasion, which resulted in the creation of extermination camps. After staging an incident, lying, and creating propaganda to justify the war, Hitler invaded Poland, vastly outnumbering the Polish. With the help of the Soviet troops, Poland was not able to stand against the invasion.

The Nuremberg Trials

After the war, the top leaders in Germany were tried for the crimes of the country, including war crimes, crimes against peace, crimes against humanity, and conspiracy. Of the 199 defendants tried, 161 were convicted and 37 were sentenced to death. Generally, the defendants would admit that the crimes had taken place but said that they were not responsible as they were following higher orders. Since Hitler had committed suicide, he was not able to be tried at the trials, along with several of his closest aides who had done the same. This was not the only trial of German leaders, as many others were tried in Germany and in different countries.

Analysis: Was Hitler's rise to power inevitable?

 Although the conditions were sufficient for his rise to power, we can never historically say that Hitler’s rise to power was inevitable because there are so many possibilities for everything. Hitler had the right skills to take control of Germany, as he was good at using propaganda and had good oratorical skills. He was able to be noticed by the public due to those things, and then gained their interest through exploiting the unrest caused by the Great Depression. Hitler became chancellor in January 1933 and a series of events, including the Reichstag fire and the Enabling Act gave him full power, letting him later become the only leader (Führer). As Hitler didn’t actually win the election where he became a leader and instead was given power by the person who was elected, his rise to power could have been prevented from the elected leader choosing someone else to give the power to. There are many other theories that people have made of how Hitler’s rise to power could have been practically prevented.
 Hitler chose to target the Jews in mass exterminations for his “Final Solution to the Jewish Question” for a few reasons. First of all, he had been influenced by the policies of many leaders that he had followed. Second, he saw the Jews as a people that could be blamed on what he believed to be wrong with the world. In addition, the Jews were not the only people group that Hitler targeted - he tried to exterminate many minority groups which he deemed “inferior” to the German people.
